Project Description

The SUIGENKYO website design project was developed for Information Design at SFU's Interactive Arts and Technology program. The goal was to enhance the webpage by bringing the craftsmanship behind the products to the forefront, aligning with the corporation's core mission.

By identifying design challenges, optimizing user flow, analyzing shopping behaviors, incorporating content marketing strategies, and applying visual principles, we refined the website to create a smoother, faster, and more engaging purchasing experience. The redesign also introduced new ways for customers to explore the artisans' untold stories, the history and materials behind their products, and their dedication to their craft.

My Main Tasks in This Project:

  • Optimizing User Flow
  • UI Design Choices
  • Website Prototype, Graphics, and Effects

Optimizing User Flow

As a team we observed that the brand's cultural heritage and craftsmanship were its strongest assets, yet the original site buried these elements behind cluttered navigation. To address this, I created wireframes that stripped out redundant steps and simplified the layout. I placed craftspeople profiles and product stories at the forefront of the structure and used section breaks and image placeholders to highlight cultural elements, ensuring they were integrated into the browsing experience from the start.

Building on the wireframes, I mapped the user flow to make navigation intuitive and consistent with the brand's identity. I mapped the flow so users could move easily between product, brand, and craftsman pages while still engaging with the cultural narratives behind each item. These decisions made the browsing experience smoother and encouraged users to spend more time exploring, deepening their appreciation of both the craftsmanship and the cultural context of the products.

UI Design Choices

When reviewing the original site, I noticed cluttered navigation, hidden craftspeople profiles, and a lack of interactive features. To address this, I applied principles such as Hick's Law, the Law of Proximity, and the Peak-End Rule. I simplified navigation by keeping only essential pathways including products, craftspeople, and checkout, redesigned the craftspeople page with names, portraits, and stories for stronger impact, and introduced crafting-process videos along with an interactive map of product origins.

These design choices shifted the website away from feeling like a generic commercial catalog and revealed the brand's authentic cultural heritage, giving visitors the storytelling and context they seek when exploring handcrafted products. This transformation made the site more meaningful and engaging, strengthening the connection between customers and the brand.

Website Prototype and Interactions

I developed the full interactive prototype in Figma, covering every page and interaction from navigation to checkout. To make browsing more engaging, I implemented microinteractions such as hover-based image galleries for products, expandable craftsman profiles with names and portraits, and clickable lists for direct access. Along with other interactive refinements, these choices turned the prototype into a functional simulation that let users explore cultural details more naturally, resulting in a shopping experience that felt immersive and memorable.

Takeaways

This project was a turning point in how I think about design, pushing me to refine my ability to structure user flows, build meaningful visual hierarchies, and express cultural values through interfaces. Through this experience, I began to see websites as narrative spaces where every layout choice, from the placement of a portrait to the timing of a video, contributes to the story users take away. This shift has changed how I approach design: not just as a way to organize information, but as a way to shape experiences that connect and resonate.
